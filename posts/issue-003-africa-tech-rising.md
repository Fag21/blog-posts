## Something Happened in Addis Ababa Last Month

A startup called Dodai — founded three years ago, operating right here in Addis Ababa — just raised **$13 million** in a Series A round.

They build electric motorbikes and battery-swapping infrastructure. They've deployed over 2,000 electric motorcycles on Ethiopian streets. 97% of their 100 employees are Ethiopian. And they just attracted backing from British International Investment and a group of Japanese investors.

The round was announced on April 28, 2026.

I live in this city. I passed their bikes on the road before I knew what the company was.

That hit different.

![Electric motorcycle on a busy African city street](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1558618666-fcd25c85cd64?w=1200&q=80)

## The Bigger Picture: African Tech Right Now

Dodai isn't an isolated story. It's a data point inside a much larger shift.

African tech startups raised $382 million in Q1 2026 — up 35% on the same period last year. Broader estimates tracking total funding including debt put Q1 2026 between $597 million and $705 million — a 27% increase over Q1 2025, signaling that the funding winter that gripped Africa's tech ecosystem through 2023 and 2024 may finally be thawing.

Africa's technology story is also evolving beyond its celebrated fintech roots. A new wave is emerging — AI infrastructure, IoT platforms, robotics systems, edge computing, smart agriculture, assistive devices, and localized AI models designed around African realities.

Google for Startups Accelerator Africa received nearly 2,600 applications for its latest cohort — selecting 15 AI-focused startups building solutions in fintech, agri-tech, e-health, mobility, and SaaS.

Qualcomm's 2026 Make in Africa Mentorship Program selected 10 deep-tech startups from more than 1,200 applicants across 45 countries.

The numbers are real. The momentum is real.

## But Here's the Part Nobody Wants to Say

AI-related VC investment doubled to $259 billion last year from 2023, with three-quarters of it flowing to US companies.

African tech funding is becoming more dependent on regional investors and development finance institutions as global investors prioritize AI-driven opportunities in developed markets, reducing their exposure to frontier economies.

In Q1 2026, debt and hybrid financing instruments accounted for approximately $490 million of the $705 million tracked — roughly 70% of total capital. Pure equity funding came to approximately $212 million.

So here is the honest read: Africa raised more money, but a larger share of it is debt, not equity. Global VC is chasing AI infrastructure in the US. The international capital that used to flow freely into African startups is being redirected.

![Data visualization showing global VC funding flows](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1551288049-bebda4e38f71?w=1200&q=80)

This is the complicated truth that gets lost between the optimistic headlines and the doom narratives. Both are partially right. Neither tells the full story.

## What Dodai's Story Actually Teaches

The reason Dodai is worth studying isn't just the $13 million. It's the *choices* they made to get there.

While competitors piled into Kenya, Nigeria, and South Africa, founder Yuma Sasaki made an unconventional choice — setting up in Addis Ababa, a market most international investors considered too difficult.

Ethiopia introduced one of the most aggressive electric mobility policies on the continent, including a ban on the import of fuel-powered vehicles. Dodai saw the opportunity early — and moved first.

Hilina Legesse, SVP at Dodai, put it this way:

> *"Every constraint became something we chose to take on and solve with our team and partners on the ground. We will now accelerate and scale this impact even further."*
> — Hilina Legesse, SVP, Dodai Group

That framing — *every constraint became something we chose to take on* — is the entire mental model for building in Africa.

You do not wait for the perfect conditions. You build with the conditions you have, and you become an expert in constraints that no one else has solved yet.

That expertise becomes your moat.

## What This Means for Me — And Maybe for You

I'm a software engineering student in Addis Ababa. Not building hardware. Not raising Series A rounds anytime soon.

But what Dodai proves is that the city I live in is not a limitation. It's a context. And the right founder — with the right insight into that context — can build something that attracts international capital, creates 100 local jobs, and puts 2,000 vehicles on the streets.

The global AI boom is pulling investment away from Africa right now. That's real. But it's also creating a gap — problems that need localized solutions, markets that global companies won't touch because they don't understand them, users who need products built by people who live among them.

![African developer working on laptop with city skyline](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1522202176988-66273c2fd55f?w=1200&q=80)

I live among them. So do you, if you're reading this from somewhere the tech world calls "frontier."

That's not a disadvantage. That's proprietary knowledge.

The question is what you build with it.
